Research Overview


I am broadly interested in research topics pertaining to near-field cosmology, and am particularly excited for future missions and instruments that (1) expand our census of nearby satellites, and (2) permit us to conduct more detailed spectroscopic studies of their various properties. I am also looking forward to working with increasingly detailed simulations that such observational developments will motivate. In the future, I hope to work at the interface of theory and observations to gain a deeper understanding of the nature of dark matter, as well as how galaxies evolve.

My work so far has been chiefly on individual Milky Way satellites. I am currently studying the Crater II, Hercules, and Sagittarius II Milky Way satellites. In the past, I have studied the 300S stream, and the Ursa Minor dwarf galaxy. My work on the 300S stellar stream has been accepted for publication in the Astrophysical Journal.

The above figure is the "Field of Streams," a matched-filter map made from SDSS photometry taken in the northern hemisphere. The color corresponds to the distances of the stars. Milky Way halo substructure are labeled, and circles designate the location of Milky Way satellites discovered in SDSS. Studying these substructure and stellar associations in our Milky Way can help us unravel pertinent cosmological questions, and those surrounding galaxy formation and chemical enrichment-- hence the name "near-field cosmology."

Specific Projects

Current Projects

Dynamical Histories of the Crater II and Hercules Dwarf Galaxies

Crater II and Hercules are two Milky Way dwarf galaxies for whom tidal interactions have been posited as possible causes of their peculiar structure. Recently-published proper motions by Gaia DR2 allow us to test this hypothesis. In this work, I test the tidal stripping hypothesis for both of these galaxies using a combination of spectroscopic and astrometric data. Using Magellan/IMACS data, I measure the chemodynamics of Crater II, and attempt to search for Hercules members beyond the dwarf's tidal radius. Using Gaia DR2 astrometry, I determine whether the pericenter distance of each dwarf galaxy's orbit is sufficiently small for the galaxy to have been tidally stripped by the Milky Way. I am in the process of summarizing the results of this work for publication, and will be presenting this as a poster presentation at the 233rd AAS meeting in Seattle, WA.

The Nature of the Peculiar Milky Way Satellite, Sagittarius II

What makes a dwarf galaxy, and what makes a globular cluster? Traditionally, these classifications could be made because dwarf galaxies and globular clusters occupy separate planes in size-luminosity space. However, advancements in the discovery of dwarf galaxies particularly in the last decade are challenging such classification regimes. Sagittarius II, discovered in Laevens et al. 2016, is one of such satellites, sitting right in the middle of the dwarf galaxy and globular cluster planes. In this work, I use Magellan/IMACS and Magellan/MIKE spectra to determine the classification of Sagittarius II. I will be presenting preliminary results of this work at the 233rd AAS meeting in Seattle, WA as an iPoster presentation.

Past Projects

The Origin of the 300 km/s Stream Near Segue 1

The kinematic signature of 300S was first discovered in spectroscopic studies of the Segue 1 dwarf galaxy as a separate stellar population moving at 300 km/s. In this work, I use APOGEE-2 and SEGUE survey data to map out the 300S stream. We find that 300S is likely the remnant of a tidally disrupted dwarf galaxy akin to the classical Milky Way dwarfs. Since this work happeend prior to the second Gaia data release, I determined the orbit of the stream by inferring its proper motions from inferred stream observables. As one might expect from a heavily tidally disrupted system, we find that the stream is on a highly eccentric orbit, with a pericenter distance of 5 kpc. This work is now published in the Astrophysical Journal.

The Chemical Evolution of the Ursa Minor dSph

The Ursa Minor (UMi) dwarf galaxy is one of the 6 classical dwarf spheroidal galaxies observed by the APOGEE-2 survey. For this project, I attempted to analyze the chemical evolution of this satellite using the proto-DR14 APOGEE-2 reduction and analysis. I found discrepancies between the chemical abundance measurements of UMi stars from APOGEE-2 and from literature. After some more investigation, it turns out that these discrepancies were in part caused by faulty timeseries velocity measurements of the dwarf. Click here for a PDF of my poster presentation for this work, which I presented at the 229th AAS meeting in Grapevine, TX.

About me

I am currently a senior at Pomona College. The liberal arts component of my education has been important to informing my sense of responsibility as an astronomer and a scientist. Within the academy, this means that I feel a particular responsibility to supporting aspiring astronomers and physicists from marginalized backgrounds. My other academic interests include thinking about the ways in which history, sociology, gender & womens' studies, ethnic studies, literature, and their various intersections (with each other, with science), can inform the ways in which we build a more equitable and inclusive society.
